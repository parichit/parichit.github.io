01What's in the folder

Your site is just a handful of plain files — no build step, no server, nothing to install.

FileWhat it is
content.jsThis is the file you edit most. All your words, links, and papers — for every page.
index.htmlHome page (hero + About + Research + Contact). Edit its title/SEO tags once.
publications.html
teaching.html

site.cssThe design system (colors, type, spacing). Rarely needs touching.

assets/Your photo, CV PDF, and any project images go here.
One data file, many pages Your site has separate pages for Publications, Teaching, and Writing, plus a Home page that carries About, Research and Contact. All of them pull from the same content.js — edit once, every page updates. Your CV link opens assets/cv.pdf straight in the browser.
The golden rule To change what the site says, edit content.js. To change how it looks, use the Design studio (next section) or site.css.

02Editing your content

Open content.js in any text editor (even Notepad / TextEdit works, but a free editor like VS Code is nicer). You'll see clearly-labelled sections. Change the text between the quotes, save, and refresh your browser.

Four rules so nothing breaks

  • Keep the "quotes" around every piece of text.
  • Keep the comma at the end of each line inside { } and [ ].
  • To add an item (a paper, a news entry), copy a whole { … } block — from one { to its matching } plus the comma — and paste it below.
  • To hide a whole section, set its enabled: true to enabled: false.

Two handy formatting tricks

Inside news items and bios you can use:

// bold text:
"Our paper was accepted to **ICML 2026**."

// a link:
"Read more on [my blog](https://example.com)."

Writing a real blog post

The blog section in content.js holds two kinds of entry:

  • A full on-site post — give it a slug (a short web-friendly name) and a body. It opens as its own page at post.html?p=your-slug with a proper reading layout, automatic word count & reading time, and footnotes.
  • An external link — give it a link instead of a body, and it simply opens that URL (handy for guest posts).

A post body is a list of simple blocks — you mix and match:

body: [
  { h: "A subheading" },
  { p: "A paragraph with **bold**, *italic*, [links](url), and a footnote[^1]." },
  { quote: "A pulled-out quote or a counter-argument." },
  { list: ["first point", "second point"] },
  { code: "pip install scquant", lang: "bash" },
  { image: "assets/figure.jpg", caption: "Optional caption" },
],
footnotes: [ "Defines [^1]." ],

To add a new post, copy an existing post's { … } block, paste it at the top of the list (newest first), and change the slug, title, date, and body.

If the page goes blank after an edit You almost certainly removed a quote, comma, or bracket. Undo your last change (Ctrl/⌘ + Z) and try again more carefully. Tip: edit one thing at a time and refresh, so you always know what broke it.

03Adding your photo & CV

  1. Put a square-ish headshot in the assets folder named portrait.jpg.
  2. Put your CV in assets named cv.pdf.
  3. (Optional) Add research-1.jpg, research-2.jpg, etc. for project thumbnails, and a og.jpg (1200×630) for nice link previews when you share the site.

That's it — the site picks them up automatically. Until you add them, tasteful placeholders show in their place, so the site never looks broken.

04Choosing a design

While you preview the site here, open the Tweaks panel (the toggle in the toolbar) to see the Design studio. Flip between hero layouts, accent colors, sans/serif headings, density, and dark mode — live.

When you find a combination you love, click “Copy theme settings” and paste the block it gives you over the theme: { … } block at the top of content.js. That locks it in for your real site.

Why you won't see Tweaks on the live site The Design studio only loads inside this preview. Your published site stays lean and fast — no extra code is ever sent to visitors.

05Publishing on GitHub Pages

GitHub Pages hosts static sites free, forever, at https://USERNAME.github.io. Here's the whole path from zero.

1
Create a free GitHub account at github.com/signup. Pick a username you're happy to show publicly — it becomes part of your web address (e.g. parichit.github.io).
2
Create a new repository named exactly USERNAME.github.io (replace USERNAME with your username, all lowercase). Set it to Public. This special name is what makes it your main site.
3
Upload your files. On the new repo's page click “uploading an existing file”, then drag in everything from your site folder — all the .html pages (including post.html), content.js, site.css, render.js, design-mode.js, and the assets folder. Click Commit changes.
4
Turn on Pages. Go to Settings → Pages. Under “Build and deployment”, set Source to Deploy from a branch, choose the main branch and / (root) folder, and click Save.
5
Wait ~1 minute, then visit https://USERNAME.github.io. Done — your site is live on the internet. 🎉
Prefer not to use the command line? You never have to. Everything above works through GitHub's website with drag-and-drop. The free GitHub Desktop app is an even smoother way to upload changes if you'd like one.

06Updating the live site later

Any time you publish a paper, add news, or tweak your bio:

  1. Edit content.js on your computer and confirm it looks right by opening index.html locally.
  2. On your repo, open the file (or the assets folder), click the pencil ✏️ / upload, drop in the new version, and Commit changes.
  3. Your live site refreshes within a minute. No re-publishing needed.

07Custom domain (optional)

The free USERNAME.github.io address is perfectly professional and many academics use it. If you later want something like parichitsharma.com:

  1. Buy the domain (~$10–15/yr) from a registrar like Namecheap, Cloudflare, or Porkbun.
  2. In your repo's Settings → Pages → Custom domain, enter your domain and Save. This creates a CNAME file in the repo.
  3. At your registrar, add the DNS records GitHub shows you (four A records, plus a CNAME for www). GitHub's step-by-step docs list the exact values.
  4. Tick Enforce HTTPS once it becomes available (free, automatic).
Remember to update your links If you move to a custom domain, change the USERNAME.github.io addresses inside index.html (the canonical, Open Graph, and JSON-LD tags) to your new domain.

08Getting discovered

A site nobody can find isn't doing its job. Here's how to make sure you show up — for your name, and for your research.

Tell Google it exists

  • Add your site to Google Search Console (free). Verify ownership, then submit your sitemap.xml. This is the single highest-impact step.
  • Your folder already includes a sitemap.xml and robots.txt — just update the USERNAME.github.io address inside them.

Connect your scholarly identity

  • Put your website URL in your Google Scholar profile, ORCID, LinkedIn, your university page, and your email signature. Every link pointing to your site raises it in search results.
  • Keep the links in content.js accurate — they're also written into the site's structured data, which is what can earn you a Google “knowledge panel”.

Make sharing look good

  • Add an assets/og.jpg (1200×630). It's the image people see when your link is shared on X, LinkedIn, or Slack.
  • Use a clear, keyword-rich <title> and description in index.html — e.g. your name plus your field. That's the text Google shows in results.

Keep it fresh

  • Update the News section whenever something happens. Active sites rank better and signal you're, well, active.
  • Writing the occasional blog post gives Google more pages to index and more reasons for people to link to you.
